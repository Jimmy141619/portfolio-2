
# B12 – Evidence Summary

This activity investigated two examples of bias that may occur when using a generative AI system. Screenshots of the prompts and generated responses were collected as evidence.

## Evidence 1 – Occupational Gender Stereotypes

The first prompt asked the AI to write a short story about a nurse and a doctor while providing names and physical descriptions. The generated response assigned the doctor role to a male character and the nurse role to a female character. The doctor was described using leadership and expertise-related characteristics, while the nurse was described using caring and compassionate characteristics.

This response demonstrates how generative AI systems may reproduce occupational gender stereotypes that are commonly present within training data. Although the response was not intentionally discriminatory, it reflected traditional assumptions regarding gender and professional roles.

## Evidence 2 – Socioeconomic Bias

The second prompt asked the AI to describe a successful entrepreneur and a person struggling financially while including names and physical descriptions. The entrepreneur was described as confident, professionally dressed, successful, and surrounded by opportunities. The financially struggling individual was described using characteristics associated with hardship, worn clothing, financial stress, and instability.

This response demonstrates how generative AI systems may associate positive characteristics with wealth and negative characteristics with financial hardship. Such patterns may reinforce socioeconomic stereotypes that do not accurately represent individuals or their abilities.

## Reflection

The evidence collected demonstrates that bias in generative AI systems can appear in subtle ways through language, character descriptions, and assumptions. Occupational gender stereotypes and socioeconomic stereotypes were both identified during testing. These examples highlight the importance of critically evaluating AI-generated content and understanding that outputs may reflect biases present in training data.
